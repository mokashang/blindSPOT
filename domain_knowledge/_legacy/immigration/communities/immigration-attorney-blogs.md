# Immigration Attorney Blogs

Practitioner law-firm bulletin voice — partner-and-associate writers at
mid-to-large immigration boutiques who publish regular commentary on Visa
Bulletin movement, AC21 portability, EB-1A/NIW criteria, and policy memo
interpretation. This community is a SOURCE of decision-support framings
on procedural mechanics and adjudication-trend reading; it is **not a
substitute** for case-specific licensed-counsel advice on a particular
asker's filing. The blog post tells you where the analysis lives; the
attorney-client engagement is where the binding determination happens.

## Voice

Precise, citation-oriented, written by a practicing attorney for an
audience that is half other attorneys and half informed applicants.
Sentences anchor to statute / regulation / policy-manual section
("under AC21 §106(c)", "per the February 2023 USCIS update to CSPA
sought-to-acquire", "Matter of Dhanasar three-prong"). Comfortable
saying "this is unsettled" or "service centers diverge here" — does
not claim certainty the underlying adjudication doesn't deliver.
Avoids hedging into uselessness; states the operative reading with
the date-stamp and the caveats. Murthy's bulletin reads like a
clinic; Cyrus D. Mehta's blog reads like a journal article; Boundless
reads like a consumer-explainer with a practitioner-supervised
checklist; Greg Siskind's Nation of Immigrators reads like a
policy-watcher tracking administration-level shifts.

## Mental model

- Statute, regulation, policy manual, AAO precedent, then practitioner
  consensus — in that order. A claim grounded in the USCIS Policy
  Manual outranks a claim grounded in a forum thread.
- Every benefit is administered on a *form* with a *fee* and a *filing
  location* and a *receipt-notice clock*. Procedural defects (wrong
  form edition, missing signature, incorrect fee) are recoverable;
  substantive defects (inadmissibility ground unfixable on the facts,
  prior fraud finding) are not.
- USCIS adjudication varies by service center, by adjudicator, by
  political administration; the same petition filed in 2017 vs 2020
  vs 2023 receives materially different scrutiny. Trend-reading is part
  of the practice.
- The Visa Bulletin's DFF / FAD distinction is load-bearing; reading
  the bulletin monthly is part of the job for any practitioner with
  an active employment-based or family-preference caseload.
- Self-petition routes (EB-1A, NIW under Dhanasar) are categorically
  different practices from employer-sponsored routes (PERM-based EB-2 /
  EB-3); the evidentiary discipline, the timeline, and the strategic
  posture diverge.

## Known blind spots OF this community

- **House-style optimism on "the petition is strong."** Firm blogs
  publish what works in the firm's caseload — petitions that cleared
  with the firm's evidence-build discipline, not the broader
  population's filings. Trigger: a Murthy or Cyrus Mehta post citing
  "we have seen success on EB-1A petitions with this evidence profile"
  used to calibrate an asker's own self-petition. Failure mode: the
  asker reads firm-curated success and infers a personal probability
  several standard deviations above the population RFE rate; an
  asker treating "the blog says this works" as legal advice without
  retaining counsel for case-specific assessment misallocates filing
  fees, opportunity cost, and may file a petition that draws an RFE
  that surfaces 18 months later. Recovery: treat firm-blog success
  rates as ceiling-not-floor; consult a licensed immigration attorney
  for case-specific assessment before committing to the route.

- **Date-stamp drift on policy-manual citations.** Blog posts from
  2019 / 2020 / 2022 cite policy-manual chapters that USCIS has since
  revised — most consequentially the February 2023 CSPA "sought to
  acquire" update (shifted the trigger from FAD-current to DFF-current),
  the late-2024 I-693 indefinite-validity rule, and ongoing churn on
  H-1B beneficiary-centric registration. Trigger: a firm-blog citation
  to a USCIS Policy Manual volume / chapter that pre-dates the current
  operative reading; asker pastes the blog quote into a planning
  document. Failure mode: filing strategy is built on a superseded
  reading — the asker waits for FAD-current to file under CSPA when
  USCIS now operates on DFF-current, and the child ages out. Recovery:
  verify the policy-manual chapter against uscis.gov/policy-manual/
  updates for the publication date, and confirm any procedural step
  with a licensed immigration attorney before committing.

- **In-house firm-process discipline read as generalizable advice.**
  "File the I-140 with these exhibits, in this binder order, with
  this letter template" describes a firm's internal QA pipeline, not
  a USCIS-required standard. The most-circulated EB-1A letter templates
  have produced an adjudicator skepticism that pattern-matches against
  AILA boilerplate (see `framings.md` §9 Excludes line on
  template-letter trap; `blindspots.md` §9.2). Trigger: an asker
  downloads a firm-published template, fills the blanks, and treats
  the artifact as adjudication-ready. Failure mode: a too-polished
  letter that pattern-matches the template-circulation pattern draws
  a "boilerplate language" RFE the asker doesn't know to anticipate;
  the asker self-files pro se on a template-borrowed petition and
  cannot articulate the "comparable evidence" pathway when the RFE
  asks. Recovery: treat firm templates as scaffolding to adapt to the
  asker's specific contributions in the writer's own voice; for any
  substantive self-petition, engage a licensed immigration attorney
  who reviews the full evidentiary portfolio rather than treating the
  template as the petition.

- **Modal-asker bias: writers most cite the cases they win.** A firm
  whose practice is EB-2 India backlog management writes about
  downgrade-and-interfile because that is what its caseload optimizes
  for; the same firm has thinner coverage of asylum, removal defense,
  or VAWA self-petitioning even when those are the operative pathways
  for the *blog reader's* situation. Trigger: an asker with an atypical
  fact pattern (TPS-pending, DACA, prior unlawful-presence accrual,
  VAWA-eligible) reading a blog calibrated for the H-1B-to-EB-2
  modal applicant. Failure mode: the asker concludes the firm's
  prescribed pathway (file the I-140, downgrade-and-interfile, wait
  for the bulletin) is also the pathway for their situation; the
  pathway for their situation is materially different and the firm's
  thin coverage leaves the asker without orientation. Recovery: when
  the situation pattern includes asylum, TPS, DACA, removal proceedings,
  VAWA-eligibility, prior unlawful presence, or any humanitarian-
  category overlap, route to a licensed immigration attorney with
  *that specific* sub-practice rather than generalizing from a
  business-immigration firm's blog corpus.

- **Practitioner-economic structural bias: "consult counsel" recommendation
  is itself the firm's product.** Firm blogs end with "talk to your
  attorney" not only as Mechanism-E professional-counsel deferral but
  also as marketing. The signal is real (high-stakes domain requires
  case-specific licensed-counsel advice) and the economic alignment
  is real (the firm publishes the blog to acquire clients). Trigger:
  an asker whose budget genuinely cannot absorb $8–15k of EB-1A
  attorney fees reads a firm post that says "EB-1A self-petition is
  feasible; consult an attorney." Failure mode: the asker concludes
  "EB-1A is for me" because the blog is friendly toward self-petition,
  doesn't reconcile the per-hour ROI on attorney fees + 100–200 hours
  of evidence-curation against the actual priority-date arbitrage
  gain, and commits cash they can't recover. Recovery: surface the
  full direct cost (attorney fees + USCIS filing fees) and indirect
  cost (applicant evidence-curation time) before the framing's
  encouragement is acted on; confirm category-fit and the route's
  net-present-value with a licensed immigration attorney who has
  no economic stake in the petition decision, including the option
  of a modular consultation (one-hour review of route choice)
  rather than full representation engagement.
