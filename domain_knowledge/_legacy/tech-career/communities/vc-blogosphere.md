# VC Blogosphere

## Voice
Measured, founder-friendly, written by people who sit on the other side of
the cap table from the employee. Sentence rhythm is short and declarative —
Fred Wilson's blog reads like a daily memo. Comfortable with phrases like
"we like to see X% option pool at Series A". Doesn't condescend, but the
default audience is a founder or another investor, not a candidate
evaluating an offer.

## Mental model
- Equity is a coordination mechanism. Its purpose is to align founders,
  early employees, and investors over a 7–10 year arc. Compensating
  individual employees is downstream of that.
- Dilution is the central concept. Every grant, every round, every option
  pool refresh is a number on the cap table. Founders and VCs reason about
  this constantly; employees usually don't.
- Stage matters more than role. A senior engineer at seed vs Series C vs
  pre-IPO is three different deals, even at the same title.
- The right "typical" grant size is benchmarkable. There are conventions —
  0.5–1.5% for the first ten engineering hires at seed, declining by stage —
  and deviations from convention are signals.
- Liquidity events are rare and rationed. Tender offers, secondaries, and
  acquisitions are the actual events that turn paper into cash.

## Typical concerns
- **Option pool sizing pre-money** — VCs negotiate to expand the pool
  pre-money, which dilutes existing shareholders (founders + employees) but
  not the incoming investor. Standard, but consequential.
- **Refreshers and re-ups** — early employees who don't get refreshed get
  diluted to insignificance by Series C. VCs know this and budget for it;
  employees don't ask.
- **Vesting acceleration at acquisition** — single-trigger and
  double-trigger acceleration is a negotiation between acquirer and
  retention budget. Employees rarely have visibility.
- **Liquidation preference stacking** — preferred shares get paid out first
  at exit; in a soft acquisition, common (employee) shares can go to zero
  even at a 9-figure sale price.
- **Secondary sales and tender offers** — when and at what price the
  company permits employees to sell pre-IPO. Increasingly normalized at
  Series C+ but governed by ROFR and board approval.
- **Founder vesting and reverse-vesting** — what happens to founder equity
  if a founder leaves early. Signals to employees about company stability.

## Known blind spots OF this community
- **Underweights employee-level tax timing because the partnership
  tax model doesn't apply to W-2 grantees.** VCs reason at the fund
  level (carry, K-1s, QSBS); the AMT spread on ISO exercise, the
  90-day post-termination exercise-or-forfeit cliff, and the NSO
  ordinary-income hit on a tender live in a W-2 world VC writers
  rarely model. "Exercise early" gets repeated as conventional
  wisdom without naming the AMT bill or the cash requirement.
- **Pattern-matches to the YC / power-law playbook and dismisses
  lifestyle, indie, or bootstrapped paths** even when they fit the
  asker's risk profile. A $400k salary at a profitable 20-person
  bootstrapped company is invisible to a frame that scores outcomes
  against a fund's MOIC target; the community has no vocabulary for
  "good enough" outcomes that aren't venture-scale.
- **Survivorship bias toward winning portfolios.** Public VC writing
  is implicitly about deals that worked. Down rounds, recap wipeouts
  where common is washed out by participating-preferred stacks,
  acquihires that pay the team but zero out option-holders, and
  10-year stretches where the company is neither dead nor liquid are
  systematically underweighted.
- **"Early employee" collapses employees #5 and #50.** Grant sizes,
  refresher cadence, and dilution exposure differ by an order of
  magnitude between a pre-seed 1.0% hire and a Series B 0.05% hire;
  VC posts about "what early employees should expect" usually mean
  the first five hires and don't say so.
- **Big-tech / public-company RSU comp is treated as someone else's
  domain.** RSU withholding shortfalls at supplemental rates (22%
  federal vs the grantee's actual marginal), concentrated-position
  risk on a single ticker, ESPP, and 10b5-1 plans don't appear here
  even when they dominate the asker's actual decision (e.g. leaving
  a $600k TC public-company job for a Series B startup).
