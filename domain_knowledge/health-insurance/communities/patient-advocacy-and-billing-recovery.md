# Patient Advocacy and Billing Recovery

NAHAC / Patient Advocate Foundation / Marshall Allen *Never Pay the
First Bill* voice — the tactical-procedural complement to the
healthcare-economics-journalism community: where journalism
documents the structural problem, patient-advocates publish the
procedural recovery moves the asker actually executes. This
community is the **F7 framing anchor on EOB-line-item reading,
balance-billing recovery, No Surprises Act §2799A IDR-triggering,
and prior-auth-denial-appeal scripts**. It is the named-
professional layer for post-billing recovery on fully-insured
commercial plans; it is **not a substitute** for ERISA-side
litigation on self-funded plan denials (`sources.yaml` community
`erisa-and-denial-appeal-law` handles that subset), nor for
Medicare-side appeals (`sources.yaml` community
`medicare-shiba-and-government` handles QIC / ALJ / MAC navigation).

## Identity

Board-Certified Patient Advocates (BCPA — certification administered
by the Patient Advocate Certification Board, PACB), members of the
National Association of Healthcare Advocacy (NAHAC), Patient
Advocate Foundation case managers, AdvoConnection-directory member
advocates, and the lineage of consumer-side billing-recovery
methodology built by Marshall Allen (*Never Pay the First Bill*,
2021; pre-death ProPublica investigative work cataloging EOB-
reading and balance-billing recovery patterns). They reason about
healthcare billing and denial-appeal as a *procedural workflow*
problem: every EOB has CPT codes that can be looked up; every
chargemaster price has a UCR comparison; every prior-auth denial
has an appeal procedure with named timelines and a documented win-
rate by the procedural step taken; every surprise bill from an out-
of-network provider at an in-network facility has a No Surprises
Act §2799A IDR-arbitration triggering procedure (the asker must
know to request it; facilities don't volunteer the option). They
are *not* the journalism community (advocates don't write the
investigative-pattern recognition; they execute the procedural
recovery); they are *not* ERISA attorneys (advocates handle the
pre-litigation procedural phase on fully-insured plans; ERISA
litigation is downstream and a different specialty).

## Mental model

Healthcare billing is a procedural workflow with documented
recovery moves at each friction point, executable by anyone who
knows the procedure — but the procedure is asymmetrically
documented (insurers and providers know it; patients largely
don't). The first principle: *never pay the first bill* — the
first bill is the chargemaster price, not the negotiated rate,
not the EOB-determined allowable, not the post-§2799A IDR rate;
matching the EOB to the bill is the first procedural move. EOB
reading: every line item has a CPT (Current Procedural
Terminology) code, the provider-billed amount, the carrier-
allowed amount, the patient-responsibility amount, and a
remark code; lookup the CPT against the FAIR Health Consumer
UCR database to see the local-area fair price, and compare the
provider-billed amount to it. Surprise-billing recovery: under
No Surprises Act §2799A, an out-of-network provider at an in-
network facility (or in an emergency) cannot balance-bill above
the in-network cost-sharing amount; the patient must know to
request IDR (Independent Dispute Resolution) within 30 business
days; the QPA (Qualifying Payment Amount, the median in-network
rate) is the procedural anchor. Prior-auth-denial appeal: every
denial has an appeal procedure with timing; the carrier must
provide reasons for denial in writing; medical-necessity denials
trigger external-review-process rights under ACA §2719 (with
NCQA-accredited IRO). §501(r) financial assistance: every
nonprofit hospital must publish a financial assistance policy
(FAP), have an FAP application procedure, and provide care at
charity-care rates for qualifying low-income patients — Dollar
For (the 501(c)(3)) has documented that hospitals systematically
under-promote this; the asker who applies usually qualifies.
Medical-debt-credit-reporting reform: under the 2022 Equifax /
Experian / TransUnion changes (and CFPB rulemaking), medical
debt under $500 no longer reports; paid-medical-debt no longer
reports; unpaid medical debt has a 1-year delay before reporting.

## Known blind spots OF this community

- **Workflow assumes the asker has not yet paid the bill.**
  Patient-advocate methodology is upstream of payment — once the
  asker has paid the chargemaster price, recovery is materially
  harder (refund-request procedure vs unpaid-bill negotiation).
  Trigger: asker who paid a $4,500 ER bill at the cashier's
  window in the moment, comes to a patient-advocate weeks
  later. Failure mode: refund procedure goes through provider-
  billing customer service, often takes 6–12 months, and
  recovery is partial at best. Recovery: surface the
  "never-pay-the-first-bill" principle pre-emptively — on D7 /
  D9 conversations *before* the bill is paid, route to patient-
  advocate / EOB-matching procedure; never recommend paying the
  first bill in full.

- **No Surprises Act IDR procedure is patient-side initiated
  but post-2022 enforcement has shifted to provider-side wins
  on QPA methodology challenges.** TMA I/II/III court rulings
  have invalidated several CMS interpretive rules on QPA
  computation; the IDR provider's role has become provider-
  side-friendly relative to statute. Trigger: asker triggers
  IDR expecting a clean dispute-resolution; the IDR provider
  finds in favor of the out-of-network rate, not the QPA.
  Failure mode: asker pays the higher arbitrated rate; the
  procedural path looked clean on paper. Recovery: at any NSA
  procedural-rights conversation, name the post-TMA enforcement
  reality; the consumer-protection is real but enforcement is
  procedurally contested, and patient-advocate help on the
  filing-strategy materially improves outcomes vs solo-asker
  filing.

- **§501(r) financial-assistance variability across hospital
  systems.** Federal §501(r) requires nonprofit hospitals to
  publish a financial-assistance policy, but the *generosity* of
  the policy varies wildly — some hospitals offer 100% charity
  care at 400% FPL, others 100% at 200% FPL with sliding
  scale to 400%, others only 100% at 100% FPL. The asker
  doesn't always know to check the specific hospital's FAP,
  and patient-advocate materials sometimes generalize
  generously. Trigger: asker reads "you may qualify for charity
  care at any nonprofit hospital" and assumes their hospital's
  FAP is comparable. Failure mode: asker's hospital's FAP is
  restrictive; recovery is partial. Recovery: at any §501(r)
  conversation, look up the *specific* hospital's FAP (every
  nonprofit hospital must publish it on their website per
  §501(r)(4)); Dollar For maintains a searchable database for
  many systems.

- **Prior-auth-denial-appeal scripts portable across plans but
  not across self-funded-vs-fully-insured.** Patient-advocate
  procedural advice routinely names "appeal under your plan
  documents" but the procedural rights differ materially:
  fully-insured plans answer to state insurance commissioner +
  ACA §2719 external review + carrier internal appeal; self-
  funded ERISA plans answer to 29 CFR §2560.503-1 + ACA §2719
  external review + §502(a) federal civil action. Trigger:
  patient-advocate script that doesn't differentiate. Failure
  mode: asker on a self-funded plan files a state insurance
  commissioner complaint that goes nowhere; ERISA's 180-day
  appeal clock runs. Recovery: at any prior-auth-denial /
  medical-necessity-denial conversation, determine self-funded
  vs fully-insured first (per `erisa-and-denial-appeal-law`
  community's procedural rule); route accordingly.

- **Single-Case Agreement (SCA) leverage assumes asker has not
  already received the out-of-network service.** SCA / GAP-
  exception negotiation is pre-service leverage: the asker
  needs an out-of-network specialist for a complex condition,
  asks the carrier to grant a SCA at in-network rates before
  the appointment. Once the service has been rendered out-of-
  network, the SCA leverage evaporates (the carrier has no
  reason to grant retroactively). Trigger: asker who received
  out-of-network specialty care, was balance-billed, and asks
  patient-advocate for SCA recovery after the fact. Failure
  mode: SCA is denied; balance-billing remains. Recovery:
  before any out-of-network specialty care, run the SCA / GAP-
  exception request through the carrier; document network-
  adequacy gap (specialist's in-network availability /
  geographic accessibility) as the SCA justification.

- **Medical-debt-credit-reporting reform has narrowed but not
  eliminated the harm.** The 2022 credit-bureau changes and
  CFPB rulemaking removed medical debt under $500, removed
  paid-medical-debt, and added a 1-year reporting delay — but
  unpaid medical debt above $500 still reports after 1 year;
  collection-agency calls and lawsuits proceed regardless of
  credit-reporting status. Trigger: asker reads about the
  credit-reform and concludes medical debt no longer matters.
  Failure mode: asker ignores a $5,000 surgical bill, debt
  goes to collections, asker is sued by the collector, has no
  procedural defense. Recovery: even under the credit-reform,
  unpaid medical debt produces collection / lawsuit risk;
  procedural recovery (EOB-matching, FAP-application, payment-
  plan negotiation) remains essential, not optional.

## Mechanism E posture

Health-insurance is `high_stakes: true` per `_meta_ontology.md` §4.
Every blindspot above ends with a Recovery move routed to a named
professional channel. For patient-advocacy-and-billing-recovery-
sourced framings the default channel is a **patient-advocate post-
billing recovery (NAHAC member / BCPA / Patient Advocate Foundation
case manager) for fully-insured commercial-plan claims under No
Surprises Act §2799A IDR mechanics** — this community's named-
professional layer is the procedural-recovery specialist. Where
the plan is **self-funded ERISA**, Mechanism E re-routes to
**ERISA-side plaintiff attorney** (`sources.yaml` community
`erisa-and-denial-appeal-law`); where the asker is on **Medicare**,
to **SHIBA / SHIP counselor + Center for Medicare Advocacy**
(`sources.yaml` community `medicare-shiba-and-government`); where
the claim involves a **nonprofit hospital's bill above the asker's
likely FAP qualification**, to **Dollar For (the §501(r) charity-
care navigation 501(c)(3))** in addition to the patient-advocate;
where the carrier is on **a fully-insured plan and refuses to
respond**, to the **State Insurance Commissioner** complaint
procedure (NAIC member directory; `sources.yaml` community
`broker-and-actuarial`). This community is the EOB-reading,
balance-billing-recovery, and NSA-IDR-procedure layer; the named
professional with the asker's specific bills, EOBs, plan documents,
and appeal correspondence is the binding-determination layer.
