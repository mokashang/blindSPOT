# Climate and Insurance Research

The physical-risk-and-insurance-loss-data voice — First Street
Foundation's parcel-level climate-risk research, IBHS (Insurance
Institute for Business & Home Safety) wind / hail / wildfire /
hurricane testing data, state insurance commissioner publications,
and the climate-resilience inspector / building-science research
community. This community is a SOURCE of forward-looking flood /
wildfire / heat / wind / hail trajectory, FORTIFIED-roof and
mitigation-construction ROI data, insurance non-renewal and
premium-escalation trajectory by metro and peril, and state
residual-market and non-admitted-carrier context. Per
`domain_knowledge/housing/blindspots.md` (`high_stakes: false` for
housing overall, BUT F8 climate-and-insurance is one of the
designated tail-risk framings where professional counsel is named
inline), the recovery posture is: self-directed pre-screen using
First Street / IBHS / state commissioner data, ESCALATING TO a
licensed insurance broker + IBHS-trained / climate-resilience
inspector for any purchase in a high-trajectory peril zone before
the inspection contingency expires.

## Voice

Model output, peer-reviewed research, and trade-association
testing data. First Street's publications read like climate-risk
research papers with a public-facing summary on top — methodology
section (NOAA inputs, downscaled regional climate model, hydraulic-
hydrologic modeling for flood, fire-spread modeling for wildfire),
30-year forward projection, parcel-level risk rating (1–10 scale on
each peril). IBHS reports read like a building-science trade
publication — wind-test results from the IBHS research center in
Chatsworth, SC; FORTIFIED building-standard documentation with
premium-discount mechanics; mitigation-cost-benefit studies on
roof-deck taping, ring-shank nailing, opening protection.
State insurance commissioner publications read as regulatory
press releases plus enforcement actions — non-renewal-rate data,
state residual-market participation numbers, rate-filing approvals
and denials, fair-plan or beach-plan or wind-pool capacity. The
climate-resilience inspector voice reads as practitioner-explainer
— here's what to look at on this roof, here's how to read the
parcel's flood-zone determination, here's the insurance-broker-
side question the buyer should ask before close.

## Mental model

- Climate-risk trajectory is forward-looking and parcel-level.
  Insurance pricing is backward-looking on actuarial loss
  experience but increasingly forward-looking on catastrophe-
  modeling input. The 10-year-forward insurance trajectory for a
  specific parcel can diverge from the current-year premium by a
  factor of 2–5x; the carrier's withdrawal-from-market decision
  can make coverage entirely unavailable from admitted carriers,
  routing the household to non-admitted (E&S, state residual
  market) at multiples of the admitted-market price.
- FEMA-flood-map is structurally outdated and structurally narrow
  on peril type. The NFIP map captures 100-year and 500-year
  flood-zones based on historical data; it does not capture
  urban-stormwater flood (which is increasing with intensified
  precipitation events under climate change), does not capture
  riverine-flood from changed land use upstream, and does not
  capture coastal-flood trajectory under sea-level rise. The
  First Street model captures these dimensions; the gap between
  First Street parcel-rating and FEMA flood-zone designation is a
  load-bearing diagnostic (per `blindspots.md` F8 entry on FEMA
  flood-map gap).
- IBHS FORTIFIED is the building-science discipline that
  empirically reduces wind / hail / hurricane loss. The
  FORTIFIED Bronze / Silver / Gold standards prescribe specific
  construction details (sealed-roof-deck, ring-shank nails,
  opening protection, gable-end bracing); FORTIFIED-certified
  homes have lower loss-rates in storm events AND attract
  insurance premium discounts in many storm-coast states; the
  insurance-broker-side conversation often surfaces the discount
  the asker didn't know to ask for.
- The insurance market has bifurcated into admitted (regulated,
  rate-capped, guaranty-fund-protected) and non-admitted (E&S,
  Lloyd's / surplus-lines, no rate cap, no guaranty fund). In
  high-trajectory zones, admitted-market carriers have
  withdrawn or are not writing new business; the state residual
  market (FL Citizens, CA FAIR Plan, NC Coastal Property
  Insurance Pool, TX Windstorm Insurance Association) covers
  the wind / fire / hurricane peril at limited capacity and
  often at higher cost; the rest of the coverage (HO-3 wrap, or
  E&S coverage) costs multiples of the admitted-market quote.
  Knowing which carrier-tier the household is on at year 1, 3,
  and 10 is the actual insurance question.
- Mitigation has measurable ROI in the right context. IBHS data
  shows roof-deck sealing and ring-shank nailing reduce wind
  losses by 60–80% in IBHS test conditions; FORTIFIED Bronze
  certification ($1,500–4,000 retrofit) attracts an annual
  insurance premium discount of $400–2,000 in storm-coast states
  with payback in 1–5 years; wildfire home-hardening (defensible
  space + class-A roof + mesh on vents) reduces wildfire
  destruction-probability materially in Cal-Fire / CALFIRE
  prescriptive-zone studies. The asker who treats mitigation as
  cosmetic misses the insurance-ROI argument.

## Known blind spots OF this community

- **Model-output uncertainty is rarely surfaced; forward
  projections can be over-confident on perils with weak
  ground-truth calibration.** First Street's models are best-
  calibrated where loss data is rich (riverine flood with NFIP
  claims data, coastal flood in well-instrumented zones, wildfire
  in CALFIRE-mapped zones). They are weaker where the underlying
  physics is more uncertain or where the historical loss data is
  thin (urban stormwater in newly-developing watersheds, heat-
  stress trajectory for human-occupant comfort vs structural
  damage, wind-trajectory under shifting hurricane tracks, hail
  trajectory under shifting convective patterns). The parcel-
  level rating (1–10) can imply false precision; the same
  rating in two perils can have very different confidence
  intervals around it. Trigger: an asker is treating the First
  Street parcel rating as a point estimate rather than as a
  best-available-forecast with uncertainty bands. Failure mode:
  the asker either (a) buys in a low-rated zone that turns out to
  be in a peril-trajectory the model underweighted (urban
  stormwater zones in fast-developing watersheds are a current
  example), or (b) walks from a high-rated zone where the
  realized trajectory was within model uncertainty bounds in the
  more-favorable direction. Recovery (self-directed cross-
  validation): cross-reference First Street ratings against IBHS
  actual-loss data for the peril, state insurance commissioner
  non-renewal data (the carrier-withdrawal pattern is a
  forward-looking signal the carriers' actuaries have already
  priced), and the FEMA-flood-map for the FEMA-vs-FSF gap
  diagnostic; for any purchase in a high-trajectory zone, route
  the binding decision through a paid insurance broker who can
  pull current-year carrier-willingness-to-quote AND an IBHS-
  trained / climate-resilience inspector who can assess the
  property's mitigation posture and the parcel's site-specific
  risk.

- **Forward-trajectory framing can route attention away from
  current-year affordability mechanics.** The community's frame
  is forward-looking — what will this parcel's risk look like in
  10 / 20 / 30 years? The household decision often turns on a
  current-year affordability mechanic (admitted-carrier rate
  this year, can the loan close with the carrier-required wind /
  hail / fire / flood-deductible structure, does the
  lender-required hazard insurance exist at all). The asker who
  reads First Street's 30-year projection and underwrites the
  trajectory may still miss the current-year question: is there
  an admitted-carrier willing to write the policy at all this
  year? Trigger: an asker has read the climate-risk research,
  has the forward-trajectory framed, AND is making the offer
  without a current-year insurance-broker quote in hand.
  Failure mode: the asker enters the contract, the lender
  requires hazard insurance for closing, the admitted carrier
  quotes come back declined or quoted at non-admitted-market
  rates that push the PITI $500–1,500/mo above the
  budget-modeled assumption; the deal either falls apart or
  closes with a budget that doesn't actually work. Recovery
  (insurance-broker-side, self-directed pre-screen): pull a
  binding insurance quote BEFORE the inspection contingency
  expires, not at the financing-contingency-expiration step;
  for any parcel with a First Street rating ≥ 4 on any peril or
  any address in a known-non-renewal zone (FL coast, CA-WUI, TX
  hurricane coast, parts of NC / SC / LA / MS coast, parts of
  CO / NM / AZ WUI), retain a licensed insurance broker familiar
  with the state's residual market and non-admitted-market
  pathways before going to contract.

- **Mitigation-ROI framing assumes the household will execute
  on the mitigation; many won't.** The IBHS data on FORTIFIED-
  Bronze retrofit ROI ($1,500–4,000 retrofit, $400–2,000 annual
  premium-discount payback) is real and well-calibrated for
  households that will actually retrofit. Many households who
  read the mitigation literature do not execute — the roof
  isn't due for replacement yet (FORTIFIED-Bronze re-roof is
  the natural moment), the discretionary cash isn't there, the
  contractor competency for FORTIFIED installation isn't
  available in the local market, or the household sells before
  the retrofit pays back. Trigger: an asker is modeling the
  household insurance trajectory assuming a planned FORTIFIED-
  Bronze retrofit at year 3 or 5. Failure mode: the retrofit
  doesn't happen on the planned schedule; the trajectory the
  household underwrote at purchase no longer holds; the
  household either pays the non-mitigated rate continuously or
  retrofits late after a renewal cycle has already escalated
  to the non-mitigated rate. Recovery (self-directed planning
  with insurance-broker verification): only credit a planned
  mitigation retrofit in the household trajectory if the
  retrofit timing is tied to a natural-replacement-event already
  scheduled (re-roof due within 5 years) AND the asker has
  identified a FORTIFIED-trained contractor in the local market
  AND the insurance broker has confirmed the carrier will
  apply the discount at the asker's renewal cycle; otherwise,
  underwrite the non-mitigated trajectory and treat the
  potential retrofit as upside, not as planned.

- **State residual market and non-admitted market mechanics are
  opaque to retail readers.** The community's research surfaces
  the existence of state residual markets and non-admitted
  pathways, but the operating mechanics (what gets covered,
  what the deductible structure looks like, how the
  policy-renewal-cycle interacts with carrier-withdrawal, how a
  state residual market caps coverage at less-than-replacement-
  cost in some states) are practitioner-knowledge. The asker
  reading First Street's "FL Citizens covers wind in
  high-trajectory zones" line may not realize that FL Citizens'
  policy caps coverage at $700k structure value in most
  counties (with a higher cap in Miami-Dade and Monroe), which
  leaves a six-figure uncovered gap on properties above the cap;
  the gap has to be covered by a non-admitted wrap or self-
  insured. Trigger: an asker is buying in a state-residual-
  market-required zone (FL hurricane coast, parts of CA WUI for
  fire, parts of NC / SC coastal for wind, TX Tier 1 windstorm
  zone) above the residual-market cap. Failure mode: the asker
  is underwritten with a state residual market policy that
  caps coverage below replacement cost; in a total-loss event
  (hurricane wind, wildfire, hurricane-and-flood combined), the
  insurance proceeds don't fund full reconstruction, the
  household is materially underinsured for the disaster
  scenario the parcel was bought knowing about. Recovery
  (insurance broker for binding decision; self-directed
  pre-screen): for any purchase in a state-residual-market
  zone above the residual-market structure cap, retain a
  licensed insurance broker AND verify the residual-market
  policy limit + the non-admitted-market wrap cost + the
  total annual insurance cost BEFORE the financing
  contingency expires; for total-cost-of-ownership modeling,
  use the wrap-included annual insurance cost not the
  residual-market-base premium alone.

- **Climate-and-insurance research is structurally weak on
  metro-and-school-zone and on transactional-cycle framings.**
  This community's expertise is on the physical-risk and
  insurance-market dimensions; it is not on micro-market
  appreciation, school-zone reputation, gentrification
  trajectory, or transactional-cycle (offer-strategy, comps,
  inspection-finding triage). An asker who anchors a
  metro-selection decision exclusively in the climate-and-
  insurance trajectory may select a low-risk metro that is
  also low-economic-opportunity, or may walk from a
  high-trajectory metro where the household's specific
  property choice (inland, elevated, FORTIFIED-built,
  defensible-spaced) and mitigation posture made the
  climate-and-insurance question manageable. Trigger: an
  asker is treating the climate-and-insurance trajectory as
  the dispositive metro / property-selection input. Failure
  mode: the asker over-rotates on the climate-trajectory
  variable and underweights the metro's economic
  trajectory, school-zone reputation, commute-and-lifestyle
  fit, or the property-specific mitigation posture that
  changes the trajectory materially. Recovery (self-
  directed cross-reference): pair climate-and-insurance
  reading with `real-estate-data-and-economics` for the
  metro's economic-and-housing-cycle position, `real-estate-
  experience-forums` (metro subreddits) for the micro-
  neighborhood and school-zone reality, and the
  `journalists-and-explainers` Marketplace layer for the
  broader policy-and-mitigation-investment context; for any
  high-trajectory-zone purchase where the asker also has
  strong metro-or-property-specific reasons to consider the
  purchase, retain the insurance broker AND the climate-
  resilience inspector together to assess the parcel-and-
  mitigation-posture-specific trajectory rather than the
  zone-level default.
