# YC and VC-Backed Playbooks

The venture-track / Y-Combinator / first-principles-founder voice
— Paul Graham essays (paulgraham.com), Sam Altman essays
(blog.samaltman.com), the YC Startup School / YC Library curriculum
(Jessica Livingston / Michael Seibel / Dalton Caldwell / Aaron
Epstein), Garry Tan / Initialized commentary on the YC X-cohort
cadence, First Round Review (firstround.com/review) operator-
interview content, and the Founder Collective (David Frankel / Eric
Paley / Micah Rosenbloom) anti-venture-trap counter-voice. This
community is a SOURCE of venture-scale framings on bootstrap-vs-
raise (D2), SAFE-vs-priced-seed mechanics (D5), founder-mode-vs-
manager-mode transitions (F11 / F12), Series-A signal threshold
calibration, board-meeting mechanics, the 1-in-10 venture-portfolio
math, and the founder-as-product-manager-of-the-company posture.
It is **not a substitute** for case-specific advice from a retained
startup attorney (on D1 co-founder equity / D5 SAFE-to-priced-seed
mechanics / D11 founder-departure), S-corp-experienced CPA (on D4 /
D8), or employment-law attorney (on D7 first W-2 employee). Per
`domain_knowledge/entrepreneurship/blindspots.md` (`high_stakes:
false`), recovery here tends self-directed-first — the Paul Graham
essay, the Sam Altman post, the YC Library video, the First Round
Review interview, the Founder Collective "venture as debt" essay are
the artifacts, and the founder's own decision is the deliverable.
Professional counsel gets named inline only where the decision
carries six-figure tail risk: D1 / D5 / D11 on the startup-attorney
side, D4 / D8 on the CPA side, D7 on the employment-law side. The
YC SAFE template mechanics are visible here; the case-specific
adjudication on non-standard cap-table situations is firmly attorney
territory.

## Identity

Y-Combinator-batch founders, YC alumni, founders applying to YC,
founders raising on the YC SAFE template, and the broader VC-
backed-startup community that takes the venture-portfolio math
(~1-in-10 of seed-funded companies returns the fund at 10x+) as
the reasoning context. Heavily over-indexed in the SF / SV / NYC /
Boston / international-YC-batch geographic cohort. Reference voices
include Paul Graham's canonical essay archive (*Do Things That
Don't Scale*, *Default Alive or Default Dead*, *Founder Mode*,
*Startup = Growth*, *Maker's Schedule / Manager's Schedule*, *How
to Start a Startup*, *How to Be a Good Cofounder*); Sam Altman's
*Startup Playbook* and *How To Be Successful* essays; the YC
Library content from Jessica Livingston, Michael Seibel (How to
Plan an MVP, How to Set Goals, How to Decide What to Build),
Dalton Caldwell (How to Talk to Users), Aaron Epstein (Default
Alive); the How To Start A Startup CS183B Stanford lecture series
(now historical curriculum behind YC Startup School); Garry Tan /
Initialized commentary on YC X-cohorts and the post-AI YC cohort
composition; First Round Review's operator-interview format on
hiring-as-bottleneck, IC-to-VPE / IC-to-CRO transitions, board-
meeting mechanics; and the Founder Collective voice (David Frankel,
Eric Paley, Micah Rosenbloom) as the anti-venture-trap counter-
voice ("venture as debt" framing, capital-efficiency as
competitive moat, the anti-raise-as-much-as-you-can reflex). They
reason about the founder's-decision in venture-portfolio terms —
the company is one of N bets in a portfolio that requires power-
law-distributed outcomes; the founder's choice between bootstrap-
$2M-ARR-comfortable and venture-trajectory-toward-$100M-ARR is a
*kind* choice, not a *better/worse* choice on a single axis. They
are **not** the bootstrap-default voice (defer to `indie-hackers-
and-bootstrap` for D2 in bootstrap-default contexts), **not** the
SaaS-metric-discipline voice at the operational scale level
(defer to `saas-and-growth-economics` for ARR / NRR / CAC payback
discipline once revenue is scaling), **not** the case-specific
cap-table-mechanics voice (defer to `legal-and-cap-table-
mechanics` + retained startup attorney for D1 / D5 / D11), and
**not** the tax-mechanic voice on the founder's personal tax
treatment of the venture trajectory (defer to `tax-and-entity-
structure-cpa` + retained S-corp/startup-CPA for D4 / D8 / QSBS /
§83(b)).

## Mental model

- Capital is fuel that compresses calendar time to PMF (per
  `framings.md` F1). The framing reasons that outside capital
  enables parallel experimentation (multiple builds, multiple
  growth-marketing channels, multiple senior hires onboarded
  simultaneously) that bootstrap forces serially. Anchor: pre-
  seed $500k on $5M post = ~10% dilution; priced seed $2-4M on
  $12M post = ~17% additional dilution; Series A $8-15M on
  $40-60M post on $1-3M ARR with 3x+ YoY growth = ~25%
  additional dilution; founders doing all three rounds with no
  unusual amounts retain ~50-55% combined pre-Series-B (per
  `framings.md` F1 anchor figures).
- Venture-portfolio math drives the company-shape choice. The
  framing pre-commits to the swing-for-the-fences trajectory
  and accepts that lifestyle-business outcomes ($2-5M-ARR-
  comfortable) are *failures* relative to the implicit venture
  contract — not because lifestyle outcomes are bad
  per se, but because the venture instrument (preferred stock
  with liquidation preference, board seats, protective
  provisions) is structured around a power-law return
  distribution that requires 10x+ outcomes from the winners to
  produce fund returns. Founders who don't intend the venture
  trajectory should not take venture capital (per Founder
  Collective's "Venture Capital Is A Bummer Business").
- Default Alive vs Default Dead is the discipline. Paul
  Graham's test: if you continue your current growth-and-burn
  rate, do you reach profitability before running out of
  money? If yes, default alive (no need to raise); if no,
  raise or change the trajectory. This is the venture-voice
  bridge to the bootstrap-voice (F1 ↔ F2 conceptual bridge);
  the venture community accepts the default-alive test as
  legitimate because reaching default-alive is the precondition
  for raising from leverage rather than desperation.
- Founders should do things that don't scale, until they
  shouldn't. Paul Graham's *Do Things That Don't Scale* essay
  anchors the founder-as-recruiter-of-first-users posture: in
  the pre-PMF phase, the founder hand-recruits early users,
  hand-onboards them, hand-debugs their integration, hand-
  delivers their support — practices that *cannot* scale and
  whose entire purpose is to gather the PMF signal that scaling
  would obscure. The framing then bridges to F11 / F12 founder-
  mode-vs-manager-mode: at some point, the founder transitions
  from doing-things-that-don't-scale to managing-people-who-
  do-things-that-scale; the timing and quality of this
  transition is a recurring blindspots.md trap (F11 cluster).
- Series-A signal is a specific threshold. The YC community's
  canonical Series-A signal is $1-3M ARR with strong YoY
  growth (3x+) and net revenue retention well above 100%, in
  a category large enough to support a $100M+ revenue
  business (the "venture-scale outcome" filter). Tomasz
  Tunguz's burn-multiple framing (burn / net-new ARR < 1.0
  for a healthy Series-A-ready company) is the data-driven
  complement to the YC operator-narrative framing. The
  difference between an A-ready company and a non-A-ready
  company is qualitative AND quantitative; mis-reading the
  threshold causes founders to raise too early (down round at
  Series B when growth doesn't sustain) or too late (lose
  the round window when market conditions tighten).

## Known blind spots OF this community

- **"Raise to compress time-to-PMF" reflex assumes capital
  actually accelerates the right discovery.** The mental model
  treats outside capital as monotonically PMF-accelerating, but
  pre-PMF spend typically discovers the wrong thing faster,
  not the right thing faster (per `framings.md` F1 first
  Excludes / `blindspots.md` F1.1 cluster). Hiring 5 engineers
  to build a thing nobody wants takes 6 months instead of 18
  months but ends in the same dead-end — only with more
  engineers invested in the dead-end and a higher
  psychological cost of pivoting. Trigger: founder raised
  $1M+ pre-seed before reaching documented PMF signal (no
  10-50 customers, no $20-100k MRR, no strong qualitative
  retention signal) AND is now hiring against a 3-year burn-
  and-hiring plan that assumes the product is in the right
  shape. Failure mode: 12-18 months later, the company has
  consumed $1.5-2M with engineering team of 5-7 and no PMF
  signal; the next round requires PMF signal that doesn't
  exist; the bridge round (if any) is at a flat-or-down
  valuation with punitive terms; the team gets laid off in
  the post-bridge restructuring. Recovery (self-directed):
  apply the F2 bootstrap-default-alive discipline as a
  ruthless-prioritization framework even within a venture-
  funded company — the *what to spend on* problem at pre-PMF
  is the load-bearing one, and venture-funding doesn't
  resolve it; cross-reference with `indie-hackers-and-
  bootstrap` (Patio11 "raise your prices" / Rob Walling
  "consulting to product" discipline) and the YC voice's
  own corrective (Paul Graham's *Default Alive or Default
  Dead* + *Do Things That Don't Scale*) for the
  ruthless-prioritization framing within the venture context.

- **Founder personal-finance and reputation consequences of
  shutdown systematically understated.** The venture math
  (~1-in-10 returns the fund) requires that founders accept
  *failure of the company* as the modal outcome — but the
  founder personal-finance and reputation consequences of an
  outright shutdown are routinely understated in the swing-
  for-the-fences framing (per `framings.md` F1 second
  Excludes / `blindspots.md` F1 cluster). Failed venture-
  backed founders often face investor-relationship cost,
  board-removal-via-protective-provisions, the company-that-
  didn't-work stigma that affects subsequent fundraising, the
  multi-year delay in QSBS Section-1202 5-year-hold even on
  successful subsequent exits (because the QSBS clock restarts
  on each new C-corp issuance), and the personal-net-worth
  hit if they leveraged any personal assets (credit-card
  carryover, HELOC, personally-guaranteed business credit).
  Trigger: founder is evaluating taking the $2M pre-seed term
  sheet AND has not modeled the personal-finance and
  reputational tail-cost of the modal-shutdown outcome.
  Failure mode: at month 24 the company is in slow-decline /
  shutdown mode; founder discovers (a) board-removal-via-
  protective-provisions is a real instrument, (b) the
  shutdown takes 6-12 months of personal time with no salary,
  (c) the QSBS clock on the founder's stock is wasted (no
  5-year-hold completed at exit), (d) the post-shutdown
  funding round at the next venture company is harder because
  of pattern-matching on "founder shut down a YC company."
  Recovery (self-directed): the F13 optionality-preservation
  framing + the Founder Collective "venture as debt" essay
  are the structural correctives — explicitly model the
  shutdown scenario including the 6-12-month personal cost
  before taking the term sheet; cross-reference with
  `personal-finance/communities/financial-economics-
  journalism` for the macro-cycle context on venture
  shutdowns and `tech-career` for the post-failure-day-job-
  return-optionality analysis.

- **Institutional capital is not free in dilution arithmetic;
  protective-provision lock-in across rounds under-modeled.**
  Board seats, information rights, protective provisions
  (investor consent required for sale, debt above $X, new
  equity issuance, material change of business), and pro-
  rata side-letters compound across rounds in ways founders
  systematically under-model (per `framings.md` F1 third
  Excludes / `blindspots.md` F1.3 cluster). The framing's
  "take the best lead's term sheet" reflex routinely produces
  founders surprised at Series B by the cumulative
  protective-provision lock-in from earlier rounds. Trigger:
  founder is taking a $2-4M priced seed from a lead investor
  AND has not modeled the cumulative protective-provision
  position at Series B / C (e.g. acquisition consent threshold
  if accumulated preferred-stock > 50%, drag-along behavior
  on later rounds, pro-rata stack from multiple early
  investors with various-cap SAFEs). Failure mode: at
  Series B, founder discovers that the acquisition offer
  cannot proceed without consent from a seed investor whose
  proportional stake is now 4% but whose veto right was
  granted on the original $12M-post round; the protective
  provision in the seed term sheet was a 2-line clause the
  founder skimmed; the buyer walks. Recovery (self-directed
  pre-round; retained startup attorney for case-specific
  adjudication): apply the Founder Collective voice on
  protective-provision compounding ("Venture Capital Is A
  Bummer Business" essay) as the structural corrective; for
  any priced round, require a cap-table-and-protective-
  provision model from retained founder-side counsel (Cooley
  / Gunderson / WSGR / Orrick / Fenwick high-end; Clerky-
  coordinated attorneys + Atomic + Mintz accessible-end) per
  the `legal-and-cap-table-mechanics` community Mechanism E
  posture. Founder Collective voice catches this; YC voice
  often abstracts it.

- **Venture-funding-market cyclicality abstracted; cycle-
  dependent timing assumptions baked into framing.** The
  framing assumes a liquid venture market — but venture-
  funding availability is *cyclical* (2021 ZIRP boom collapsed
  to 2023-2024 pullback; sub-Series-A rounds in cyclical
  downturns can take 6-12 months and require 100+ investor
  meetings; the "raise quickly and focus on building"
  timeline assumes market conditions that do not hold across
  cycles) per `framings.md` F1 fourth Excludes /
  `blindspots.md` F1.4 cluster. The 24-month-runway-target-
  against-known-burn calculus needs explicit market-condition
  awareness. Trigger: founder is planning a fundraise in a
  late-cycle / declining-cycle window (Tomasz Tunguz burn-
  multiple benchmarks showing benchmark-tightening across the
  cycle; PitchBook / Crunchbase round-count and median-
  valuation data showing cycle-stage). Failure mode: founder
  plans a 24-month-runway raise assuming a 3-month fundraise
  timeline and a $X valuation; the actual fundraise takes 9
  months at a lower valuation with worse terms; the runway-
  to-Series-A is functionally 18 months not 24; the company
  runs out of runway 3 months before reaching the Series-A
  threshold. Recovery (self-directed): pair the YC framing
  with `saas-and-growth-economics` (Tomasz Tunguz cycle-aware
  burn-multiple benchmarks; SaaStr operator-narrative on
  cycle-dependent fundraising friction) BEFORE committing to
  the 24-month-runway target; in late-cycle / declining-cycle
  windows, plan for 30-36-month runway or convert to revenue-
  based-financing alternative (per `indie-hackers-and-
  bootstrap` TinySeed / Calm Company Fund framing). YC
  playbooks tend to abstract market-cycle context; SaaStr /
  Tomasz Tunguz voices catch the cyclicality.

- **YC-batch / SF-tech-network composition produces survivorship
  bias and geographic / category bias.** The YC batch
  composition is heavily over-indexed in B2B-SaaS / AI / fintech
  / dev-tools categories from SF / SV / NYC / Boston / select
  international (London / Paris / Singapore / Bangalore) ZIP
  codes; the visible success stories (Stripe / Airbnb / Coinbase /
  DoorDash / Instacart / Reddit / Twitch / Dropbox) are the
  power-law-tail outcomes that produced the venture-math
  argument in the first place. Less-visible: the failure-rate
  inside non-SF / non-network-effects / non-B2B-SaaS categories;
  the founder-burnout-and-mental-health cost of the venture
  trajectory; the disproportionate underrepresentation of
  Black / Latino / female founders in the visible-success
  bucket and the resulting under-modeling of network-access
  asymmetry. Trigger: founder is calibrating venture-track
  fit based on YC-canonical success stories AND is in a
  consumer / D2C / brick-and-mortar / services / non-network-
  effects category, OR is geographically distant from SF /
  SV / NYC / Boston, OR has identity / network characteristics
  that the YC-batch composition has historically under-
  represented. Failure mode: founder applies the YC venture-
  math reasoning to a category / geography / identity context
  where the survivorship-bias distorts the actual base rate;
  the venture-trajectory commitment locks the founder into a
  swing-for-the-fences posture that the category economics
  don't support; the founder ends up with the cost-structure
  of a venture company and the revenue-trajectory of a
  bootstrap company. Recovery (self-directed): for category-
  fit, cross-reference with `saas-and-growth-economics`
  (a16z network-effects taxonomy, Lenny Newsletter PLG
  benchmarks) AND `indie-hackers-and-bootstrap` (the
  bootstrap-default voice for non-network-effects categories
  + Founder Collective capital-efficiency-as-moat voice); for
  geography, account for the network-access cost of being
  outside the YC / SF axis; for identity / network access,
  cross-reference with community-specific founder networks
  (Black Founders / Female Founders / international-founder
  communities) for category-and-network-realistic base rates
  separate from the YC-canonical narrative.

## Mechanism E posture

  Clerky / Stripe Atlas / Atomic / Mintz accessible-end) for

  the YC voice; adjudication on non-standard liquidation-

  table buyback decisions, and any IP-assignment carve-out

  CPA) for **D4** LLC-vs-S-corp election (rarely relevant
  in pure-venture-track contexts — venture-track companies
  are Delaware C-corp by default — but relevant for the
  founder's personal-tax treatment of consulting income
  pre-formation or for any LLC-to-C-corp §351 conversion
  with QSBS implications), and **D8** retirement-vehicle
  selection at the founder personal-finance level (cross-
  domain edge to `personal-finance` on solo-401(k) / SEP-
  IRA / SIMPLE / backdoor-Roth pro-rata interaction).

  venture-track contexts but acute for any 1099 contractor

  departure with non-compete / non-solicit / IP-assignment
