# ERISA and Denial Appeal Law

ERISA-attorney + ABA ERISA Committee voice — the plaintiff-side
employee-benefits attorneys and academic-and-bar-association
publishers who specialize in 29 USC §1132 civil enforcement,
*Firestone* deference standard, 29 CFR §2560.503-1 claim-and-appeal
procedural rights, and the self-funded-vs-fully-insured pre-emption
line that determines remedy. This community is the **F3 framing
anchor on the self-funded ERISA-pre-emption blind spot** and the
binding-determination layer for D3 / D7 denial-appeal cases where
the asker's plan is self-funded. It is a SOURCE of decision-support
framings on procedural rights and circuit-level posture; it is
**not a substitute** for case-specific retained-counsel
representation on a specific denial.

*two-tier procedural* problem: (1) ERISA's claim-and-appeal procedure
under 29 CFR §2560.503-1 with specific timing requirements (180-day
appeal window from denial, 60-day plan response, document-production
right on request, independent medical reviewer requirement on
medical-necessity denials post-ACA §2719); (2) administrative
exhaustion as a prerequisite to §502(a) federal civil action, where
the standard of review (de novo vs *Firestone* deferential) is
controlled by the SPD's grant-of-discretion clause. They are *not*
the patient-advocate community (advocacy handles tactical billing-
recovery on fully-insured commercial plans; ERISA attorneys handle
the self-funded subset where state remedies are pre-empted); they
are *not* the broker community (brokers sell plans; ERISA attorneys
litigate the denials from those plans).

## Mental model

ERISA pre-emption is the load-bearing variable: a self-funded
employer health plan is governed by ERISA's federal statutory
scheme (29 USC §1001 et seq.), and ERISA §514 broadly pre-empts
state laws that "relate to" employee benefit plans — including the
state insurance commissioner's authority over the claim handling.
A fully-insured employer plan is also ERISA-governed at the plan-
participant level, but the insurance contract behind it is subject
to the "savings clause" exception that preserves state insurance
regulation of *insurance* (as distinct from plan administration)
— meaning the state insurance commissioner has jurisdiction on
fully-insured products. The self-funded-vs-fully-insured line is
not visible from the employee-experience side (both have a carrier-
branded ID card; the carrier is administering the plan in either
case); the determination requires reading the SPD's "insurance
information" section, checking Form 5500 (Schedule A vs Schedule C,
filings under §104), or asking the plan administrator directly.
Claim-and-appeal procedure under 29 CFR §2560.503-1 is procedurally
strict: the plan must respond to a non-urgent benefit claim within
30 days (extendable once for 15); medical-necessity denials require
independent medical reviewer post-ACA §2719; the participant has
180 days to appeal a denial, the plan has 60 days to decide the
appeal (extendable once for 60); the plan must provide all
documents relied upon free of charge on request. Administrative
exhaustion is generally a prerequisite to filing §502(a) federal
civil action — skipping the appeal generally bars the lawsuit. The
standard of review on federal court action is set by *Firestone
Tire & Rubber Co. v. Bruch* (489 US 101, 1989): if the SPD grants
the plan administrator discretionary authority to determine
eligibility / construe terms, the court reviews for abuse of
discretion (arbitrary-and-capricious standard); otherwise, review
is *de novo*. The grant-of-discretion clause is therefore highly
load-bearing — it is the difference between a deferential and a
fresh-look review.

## Known blind spots OF this community

- **Bar-association literature assumes the participant retains
  counsel before the appeal clock runs.** The community's
  procedural-rights detail is comprehensive but assumes the
  asker reads about it before missing the 180-day appeal window
  — and most askers find ERISA-attorney resources *after* the
  window has already tolled. Trigger: an asker who received a
  denial 200 days ago, didn't appeal (didn't know the procedure),
  reads an ERISA Law Center blog now, and discovers the
  exhaustion bar. Failure mode: claim is procedurally dead;
  *futility* exception is narrow and uncertain. Recovery: any
  D3 / D7 conversation involving a denied claim should start
  with a deadline screen — "when did you receive the denial,
  did the denial letter name an appeal deadline" — before any
  framing analysis; if the 180-day window is intact, route
  immediately to an ERISA-side attorney for case-specific
  protected appeal-letter authoring.

- **Self-funded-vs-fully-insured determination ambiguity in
  practice.** The SPD's "insurance information" disclosure is
  not always present or clear; Form 5500 filings are public
  but lag (annual filings come out the following year), and
  not every plan files (small plans below 100 participants
  often qualify for short-form or no-filing). The asker on the
  inside of an ERISA-attorney workflow may have no way to
  determine self-funded status without the plan administrator
  cooperating — which can take weeks. Trigger: asker has a
  denial, doesn't know if plan is self-funded, asks ERISA
  attorney; attorney can't proceed without the determination.
  Failure mode: weeks elapse while determination is sought;
  appeal-window clock runs down. Recovery: the *protective*
  move is to file *both* the ERISA §2560.503 appeal AND a
  state insurance commissioner complaint within the appeal
  window — if the plan is self-funded, the commissioner
  complaint goes nowhere but the ERISA appeal is intact; if
  fully-insured, the commissioner complaint adds remedy.
  Belt-and-suspenders preserves both paths.

- **Firestone-deference framing optimistic on de novo review
  availability.** ERISA-attorney blogs routinely note "if the
  SPD lacks a grant-of-discretion clause, review is *de novo*"
  but in practice nearly all modern SPDs include the boilerplate
  *Firestone* grant of discretion (post-1989, the standard
  drafting accommodation). Trigger: asker reads a 2010-era
  ERISA-attorney post on "demanding de novo review" and assumes
  the option is available. Failure mode: in court, the plan
  produces the SPD's grant-of-discretion clause; review is
  abuse-of-discretion; asker's case is materially harder.
  Recovery: read the actual SPD's discretion clause before
  building strategy on de novo review; the *conflict of
  interest weighing* under *Metropolitan Life v. Glenn* is
  the realistic lever, not absence of discretion.

- **Self-funded ERISA-plan independent-medical-review-process
  ambiguity.** ACA §2719 added an external-review-process
  requirement for non-grandfathered plans, but the
  implementation varies — self-funded plans must use an NCQA-
  accredited IRO under federal rules, but the procedural
  posture and the standards (whether the IRO reviewer is bound
  by plan terms vs medical-necessity-only) shift by plan and
  by Circuit. Trigger: asker on a self-funded plan has a
  medical-necessity denial, invokes ACA §2719 external review
  expecting a clean independent re-look. Failure mode: the
  external review affirms the plan; asker assumes this
  forecloses §502(a) (it does not — external review is in
  parallel with administrative exhaustion, and a §502(a)
  claim remains available, but procedural posture is
  contested). Recovery: at any §2719 external-review pathway,
  route to ERISA attorney to coordinate the external review
  + the §502(a) preservation simultaneously.

- **DOL EBSA enforcement is sparse.** ERISA-attorney resources
  occasionally route askers to "complain to DOL EBSA," but
  EBSA's enforcement bandwidth is structurally limited (small
  staff relative to the millions of ERISA-governed plans), and
  EBSA opens investigations on systemic / class-level concerns
  rather than individual benefit claims. Trigger: an asker
  files an EBSA complaint expecting a procedural intervention
  on their individual denial. Failure mode: weeks elapse with
  no EBSA response; appeal-window or §502(a) statute of
  limitations runs. Recovery: treat EBSA as a *systemic* lever
  (worth filing for class-relevant patterns; documents the
  pattern; may trigger Field Assistance Bulletin); for
  individual remedies, the §502(a) federal civil action
  pathway is the operative remedy.

- **Class-action and equitable-relief framework optimistic on
  individual-asker leverage.** ERISA class actions on
  systemic-denial patterns (e.g., *Wit v. United Behavioral
  Health* on mental-health-parity coverage denials, *Smith v.
  CareFirst* on residential-treatment denials) have produced
  meaningful results at the class-relief level, but the
  individual asker's leverage is limited unless their facts
  fit an active class. Trigger: asker reads about a class
  certification and assumes their individual case will be
  consolidated; counsel hasn't filed a class motion. Failure
  mode: case proceeds individually with the class-fit
  expectation slowing the procedural urgency. Recovery: for
  individual cases, focus on §502(a)(1)(B) benefits-due claim
  with case-specific facts; ERISA class actions are systemic
  reform, not individual procedural shortcut.

## Mechanism E posture

Health-insurance is `high_stakes: true` per `_meta_ontology.md` §4.
Every blindspot above ends with a Recovery move routed to a named
professional channel. For erisa-and-denial-appeal-law-sourced
framings the default channel is an **ERISA-side plaintiff
attorney (29 USC §1132 civil enforcement specialty) for self-
funded plan denial appeals (D3 / D7)** — this community's named-
professional layer is structural: ERISA pre-empts state remedies
on self-funded plans, so the state insurance commissioner is *not*
the right channel here (the broker-and-actuarial community's
default is wrong for self-funded plan denials). The attorney
handles administrative-appeal authoring within the 180-day
window, document-production demands under 29 CFR §2560.503-1, and
the §502(a) federal civil action if administrative exhaustion
produces no remedy. Where the plan is **fully-insured** (verifiable
via SPD's "insurance information" disclosure or Form 5500 Schedule
A), Mechanism E re-routes to the **state insurance commissioner
plus the carrier's grievance process** (savings-clause exception);
where the denial is on a **Medicare plan**, to **SHIBA / SHIP
counselor + Center for Medicare Advocacy / Medicare-side
attorney** (`sources.yaml` community `medicare-shiba-and-
government`). This community is the procedural-rights-and-pre-
emption layer; the named professional with the SPD, denial letter,
appeal correspondence, and current Circuit posture is the binding-
determination layer.
